These notes are a working reference I maintain while studying how modern large
language models are built and trained. They synthesize published work and my
own reading — they are **not** a claim of original research.

## Why write this down

Most "architecture" decisions at scale are really **systems** decisions: how you
shard parameters, how you schedule communication, and how you allocate a fixed
compute budget across data, model size, and training steps. Scaling laws are the
bridge that makes those trade-offs quantitative.

## Topics I am working through

### Transformer internals

Attention, normalization, positional schemes, and the trade-offs between
architectural variants (e.g. grouped-query attention, sliding windows, state-space
hybrids). The goal is to understand *why* each variant exists, not just what it
is called.

### Mixture-of-experts (MoE)

Routing, capacity, load balancing, and why sparse models change the
compute/quality curve. MoE is as much an engineering problem (all-to-all
communication, expert parallelism) as a modeling one.

### Scaling laws

How loss relates to parameters, data, and compute (Chinchilla-style analysis),
and what that implies when you have a fixed FLOP or dollar budget.

### Distributed training

Data, tensor, pipeline, and expert parallelism — plus the memory and
communication bottlenecks behind each. When does each strategy win?

### Midtraining and data mixtures

Continued pretraining, curriculum shifts between pretraining and post-training,
and how data mixture changes affect downstream capability.

### Optimization at scale

Optimizers, learning-rate schedules, loss spikes, and stability tricks that
matter when training runs for weeks on thousands of GPUs.

## What I take away so far

Writing forces clarity. A paper that says "we scale X" becomes actionable once
you can map X to a concrete design choice — batch size, parallelism strategy,
or data budget. That is the mental model these notes are building toward.
