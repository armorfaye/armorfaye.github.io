When I started working on Alzheimer's diagnosis from MRI, the natural first move
was a convolutional neural network. CNNs are excellent at spatial patterns — but
they can miss **global shape** information: how connected components merge, how
loops form around ventricles, how the topology of intensity maps changes as
tissue atrophies.

## The topological lens

Persistent homology tracks topological features (connected components, loops)
across a **filtration** — a sequence of scales at which we include more of the
data. In our OASIS pipeline:

1. Each 3D MRI volume was sliced into ~60 axial 2D images (central slices).
2. We built Vietoris–Rips filtrations on pixel intensities.
3. Persistence diagrams were aggregated into **persistence images** — fixed-size
   vectors a classifier can use.

## What we found

On 461 subjects across four severity classes (Non-Demented through Demented):

| Model | Accuracy | AUC |
|-------|----------|-----|
| Random forest (topology) | **91.8%** | **0.93** |
| AlexNet-style CNN | 86.5% | — |
| Logistic regression | 78.2% | — |

A one-way ANOVA and Tukey post-hoc tests showed the random forest was
statistically superior to both baselines (p < 0.001).

## Why this matters

The highest-importance features were interpretable: longest-lived H₀ components
and counts of significant H₁ loops — consistent with ventricular enlargement and
cortical thinning in AD. Topology gave us **biomarkers you can explain**, not
just a black-box score.

The full methods, diagrams, and limitations are in the
[NHSJS article](https://nhsjs.com/2025/topological-data-analysis-for-alzheimers-disease-diagnosis/).
