## Problem & motivation

Falls are a major health risk for older adults, yet most monitoring systems
detect a fall only after impact — too late to prevent injury. The goal of
TriFallNet was to anticipate falls early enough that a protective measure or
alert could be triggered before the person hits the ground.

## What I built

TriFallNet is an end-to-end pipeline that takes video, estimates 3D pose, and
classifies short windows of motion as *fall* or *activity of daily living* with
an explicit pre-impact early-warning objective. Alongside the model, I built a
unified benchmark so that results can be compared fairly across datasets.

## Technical approach

- **3D pose estimation.** Video is preprocessed to 25 Hz and 256×256 RGB with
  aspect-ratio-preserving letterboxing, then lifted to 3D joints with a
  MotionBERT-style estimator.
- **Kinematic feature engineering.** I derived center-of-mass velocity and
  torso inclination, which capture the dynamics of a fall more directly than
  raw joints.
- **Skeleton-MixFormer.** A transformer architecture fuses joint motion and
  kinematic features to model movement patterns over time and emit an
  early-warning score.
- **Unified benchmark.** Le2i, URFD, and GMDCSA-24 are mapped to a common
  binary taxonomy and pooled with subject-disjoint splits. A single decision
  threshold is chosen on validation and applied unchanged at test time.

> Joint-importance analysis showed that lower-body joints and torso angle are
> especially informative for early detection. Reporting includes clip-level
> metrics (F1, AUROC) and event-level metrics (event F1 under a fixed
> false-alarm budget, plus lead-time statistics).

## What's next

Generalize the framework beyond falling to analyze joint importance across a
broad range of human actions, and reduce false alarms to move toward clinical use.
