## Problem & motivation

Deaf and hearing-impaired people miss critical auditory cues — a car horn, a bike
bell, someone calling out. Existing assistive devices often detect *that* a sound
occurred but fail to answer the two questions that matter: **what** is making the
sound, and **where** is it coming from? AudioAware targets both in real time.

## What I built

A complete wearable system:

- **Smart glasses** with a four-microphone rectangular array and a camera.
- **Wristband display** showing the identified sound source and its direction
  (text + bounding-box imagery).
- **Software stack** coordinating three ML models through a FastAPI server, with
  ESP32-S3 microcontrollers handling audio/video capture and display.

## Technical approach

### Component 1 — JerryNet (direction of arrival)

A custom CNN takes a **phase matrix** built from four concurrent microphones and
predicts one of nine directions of arrival. Audio is amplified to exaggerate
inter-microphone volume differences. Trained on 450 custom samples (50 per
direction, indoor and outdoor).

### Component 2 — CLAP audio classification

Fine-tuned Contrastive Language-Audio Pretraining (CLAP) identifies the exact
sound class from audio alone, supporting zero-shot inference at deployment time.

### Component 3 — Multimodal integration

YOLOv9 generates object bounding boxes; an audio-visual localization model
(SIRA-SSL) selects the box best matching the sound using complete Intersection
over Union (cIoU). This fuses the reliable object detector with spatial audio
localization.

## Data collection

No suitable public benchmark existed, so I collected 450 four-microphone datasets
(50 per direction × 9 directions) across indoor and outdoor environments, designed
to stress-test performance under background noise.

## What's next

Scale the dataset, optimize each component for on-device inference (reducing
server dependence), and explore reinforcement learning to adapt sound-priority to
individual user preferences over time.
